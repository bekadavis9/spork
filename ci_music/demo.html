The Cochlea

Adapted from Encyclopædia Britannica, Inc. (1997) via ResearchGate

  • Each position along the 720° spiral responds to a different pitch
  • ~3,500 hair cells convert those vibrations into nerve signals

Why hearing aids don't help

90% of deafness is sensorineural — the hair cells are dead, not the ear canal.

Healthy

All cells active

Sensorineural loss

Most cells gone

Hair cells cannot regenerate. A hearing aid amplifies the signal — but if the receiver is gone, amplifying silence achieves nothing. A cochlear implant bypasses the dead cells entirely.

The Cochlear Implant

  1. 1 Microphone & processor — digitizes sound, splits into frequency bands
  2. 2 Wirelessly transmits through the skull via magnetic induction — no wires cross the skin
  3. 3 Implanted receiver — embedded in bone behind the ear — decodes and drives the electrodes
  4. 4 Electrode array stimulates the auditory nerve directly, bypassing dead hair cells

What a cochlear implant does

Normal Hearing

~3,500 hair cells
720° coverage

Cochlear Implant

4 – 24 electrodes
~450° coverage

Each electrode fires at a broad frequency range, or channel.

More channels = finer resolution

Each electrode covers a broad frequency slice. Fewer electrodes = less pitch detail.

What survives. What doesn't.

Preserved

  • Rhythm & timing
  • Loudness
  • Speech (learned over time)
  • Rough pitch (FS4 strategy)

Lost / degraded

  • Pitch detail
  • Timbre
  • Melody
  • Harmonic richness

"Rhythm is carried by amplitude envelopes — which CIs preserve.
Pitch and timbre require temporal fine structure — which CIs largely discard."
